A note on what this is

What follows is not a market guide nor a strategic proposal. These are working notes from someone who has studied the European AML problem from an engineer's angle — by reading regulatory texts, technical papers, and public enforcement decisions.

I'm interested in what works and why, not in how much it should cost or when to start. The technical sections are where I stake my professional reputation: I present them in full, with formulas, computational complexity, libraries, pitfalls. The context section is kept to the minimum needed to frame the problem, with every concrete figure cited.

Where the evidence is thin, I say so. Where I'm uncertain, I show it. I'm not selling anything in these pages.

Chapter OneThe problem in three facts

Three facts, all verifiable from public sources, define the problem anyone building AML systems in Europe is working inside. I'll line them up before getting into the technical substance.

1.1 The legislation is written. The calendar is fixed.

In June 2024 the European Union published the AML package — four legislative acts[1]: Regulation AMLR 2024/1624 (the Single Rulebook), Directive AMLD6 2024/1640, Regulation AMLAR 2024/1620 establishing AMLA, and the TFR Recast 2023/1113 extending the Travel Rule to crypto-assets. The AMLR becomes directly applicable across all Member States on 10 July 2027. AMLD6 must be transposed by the same date. AMLA assumes direct supervision of a first cohort of high-risk entities on 1 January 2028[2].

These dates are not open to interpretation. They are in the texts published in the Official Journal of the EU.

1.2 The current systems are massively inefficient

The rule-based systems that dominate the AML market produce a very high volume of false positives. The figure that circulates in the literature is 90–99% false positives, and it shows up replicated across industry studies, academic papers, and RegTech vendor communications[3]. It needs to be taken with the caution due to any number that propagates without a single original study having measured it under transparent methodology. But the qualitative point is solid: anyone working in bank compliance knows alert fatigue first-hand, and FATF[4] and Bank of England[5] publications document the inadequacy of pure rule-based monitoring against the requirements of continuous supervision.

1.3 AI is underperforming (for now)

The MIT NANDA report The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025, published in July 2025, concludes that 95% of organisations that have invested in generative AI see no measurable P&L impact[6]. The figure is based on 52 structured interviews, 153 survey responses, and analysis of about 300 public deployments. It has been criticised for sample-size limits and certain methodological choices[7], and it should be read specifically as a statement about generative AI, not machine learning broadly. That said, the number has had significant traction, and it points to a real gap between investment and returns in enterprise AI initiatives.

On the banking side, public data from the Swiss federal authority FINMA and other industry surveys[8] shows that most AI initiatives in European banking remain in pilot phase and don't reach operational deployment. The cause, consistently, is not the model — it's the data upstream.

The point in one sentence. European regulation is forcing obliged entities to adopt more sophisticated monitoring systems by 2027. Current systems produce many false positives and miss sophisticated patterns. First-generation AI replacements are struggling to reach production. The technical problem plays out in this space.

Chapter TwoThe bottlenecks on the record

Before proposing an architecture, the honest move is to name what makes AML hard. Four structural obstacles are well documented in technical and regulatory literature.

2.1 Label scarcity and label delay

Supervised machine learning systems need labelled examples: transactions or patterns where it's known with certainty whether they're laundering or not. In AML these labels are structurally scarce, and they arrive late.

A transaction flagged as suspicious (STR) is not a transaction confirmed as laundering. A case confirmed in court arrives years after the original transaction. True positive labels make up a tiny fraction of the total — academic literature on AML datasets cites ratios on the order of 0.1–0.5% of confirmed positives[9]. This creates two distinct problems: training datasets that are perpetually out of date relative to current laundering patterns, and an extreme class imbalance that breaks most standard classification algorithms.

2.2 Partial observability

Each institution only sees the transactions that pass through its systems. A layering scheme spread across three different banks is invisible to each of them taken individually. The real graph of financial flows doesn't exist physically anywhere — not even at national FIUs, which receive fragmented STRs from obliged entities with partial visibility.

This is why the 2024 AMLR introduces, for the first time, a European legal basis for sharing information about high-risk customers between obliged entities[1]. The technical modalities will be defined through AMLA's Regulatory Technical Standards.

2.3 Legacy data infrastructure

The most underrated of the four. Most traditional institutions have transaction data on legacy systems from the 80s and 90s: mainframes, AS/400, hierarchical databases, COBOL formats. This data is not "AI-ready" — it's not normalised, customer records aren't deduplicated across systems, real-time access is limited, ETL pipelines toward modern analytical environments are multi-year projects.

This is where the difference shows up between a pilot that works and a system in production. Most AI projects in banking crash on the encounter with real data quality, not on the models[6].

2.4 The regulatory explainability constraint

A compliance officer who files an STR takes on professional responsibility (and in some jurisdictions criminal liability) for the reasoning behind it. A black-box model producing "risk score = 0.87" without a decomposable explanation can't survive an audit. The AMLR and the expectations of national supervisors (FCA especially, but also BaFin and Banca d'Italia) converge on the requirement that every algorithmic decision be traceable and justifiable[10].

The EU AI Act adds a second layer: AML systems fall within high-risk categories of Annex III, with specific requirements on technical documentation, log retention, human-in-the-loop, pre-deployment validation[11].

2.5 A note on TMNL — when data sharing fails

Worth flagging a recent case that illustrates the real tensions of the problem. TMNL (Transactie Monitoring Nederland) was a joint venture between five Dutch banks that, from 2020, shared pseudonymised transaction data for joint AML monitoring. It was cited as a model for the future of cooperative AML in Europe. The wind-down was announced in July 2024[12], after the Dutch data protection authority had raised objections and after the NL-DK-DE proposal to scale it to EU level was rejected at the European table.

The technical lesson: pseudonymisation does not solve the GDPR problem. Cooperative AML platforms need a solid European legal basis, which the AMLR provides — but the technical modalities remain to be defined through RTS.

Chapter ThreeWhy a hybrid architecture

Given the picture, the question becomes: what kind of system jointly satisfies the constraints of scalability, explainability, robustness, and adequacy to known laundering patterns? No single algorithm satisfies all of them.

An approach that has gained traction in recent academic literature[13] and that is consistent with regulatory expectations is the sequential sieve: three complementary algorithmic components, each solving a piece of the problem that the others cannot.

3.1 The sieve logic

  • Layer 1 — statistical screening. Reduces the volume of transactions to monitor by identifying behaviour that's anomalous relative to the population. Works on the single entity, without seeing the network.
  • Layer 2 — relational analysis. On the suspicious entities already selected by Layer 1, propagates risk across the counterparty network. Captures relational "contagion" and multi-node patterns (round-tripping, money muling).
  • Layer 3 — explainability. Translates the numerical scores from the previous layers into reconstructable narratives — which features weighed on the Layer 1 score, which relational chains weighed on the Layer 2 score.

3.3 Why not a single deep GNN

The temptation to build a single end-to-end Graph Neural Network is real. GNNs are state of the art for many graph learning tasks[14], and the AML-specific literature is starting to build up[13]. Three pragmatic objections keep GNNs out of version 1 of the system:

  • Explainability isn't there yet. Explainability techniques for GNNs (GNNExplainer, SubgraphX, PGExplainer) exist[15] but are still fragile, and translating their explanations into compliance language is hard. Building explainability inside the architecture is structurally more solid than bolting it on afterwards.
  • Adversarial robustness. Bypassing a single model, however sophisticated, is a known problem for an attacker with resources. A three-layer sieve multiplies the conditions needed for a bypass.
  • Co-existence with legacy systems. A module that plugs into existing transaction-monitoring systems via API, rather than replacing them, has a more realistic adoption path than a platform that wants to displace the rule-based engine.

GNNs remain on the table for a version 2 of the system. For now, the algorithms in the sieve are explainable by construction, and that's a value in itself under a regulatory constraint.

Chapter FourLayer 1 — Isolation Forest

Isolation Forest is an unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm proposed by Liu, Ting, and Zhou in 2008[16]. The idea is disarmingly simple: instead of modelling the distribution of normal data (hard in high dimensions), it models the ease of isolating a point. Anomalous points isolate in few cuts; normal points sit in the mass and require many cuts to be isolated.

4.1 How it works

The algorithm builds an ensemble of trees (typically 100), each trained on a subsample of $\psi$ points (default 256). Each tree is built recursively: at each node, a random feature is picked and a random split value within the observed range for that feature. The point "isolates" when it lands in a leaf with a single element.

The average isolation path length across the ensemble is the proxy for anomaly.

4.2 The score formula

$$s(x, n) = 2^{-E(h(x))/c(n)}$$

Where:

  • $E(h(x))$ is the average path length needed to isolate point $x$, computed across the tree ensemble.
  • $c(n)$ is the normalisation factor depending on the subsample size $n = \psi$. Technically, it's the average length of an unsuccessful search in a binary search tree of $n$ elements: $c(n) \approx 2 \cdot H(n-1) - 2(n-1)/n$, where $H(k)$ is the $k$-th harmonic number.
  • $s(x, n)$ is the anomaly score, normalised to $[0, 1]$.

Reading the value: $s \approx 1$ indicates strong anomaly (the point isolates with few cuts); $s \approx 0.5$ indicates ambiguous behaviour; $s \approx 0$ indicates normal behaviour.

4.3 Computational complexity

For an ensemble of $N$ trees with subsample size $\psi$:

  • Training: $O(N \cdot \psi \log \psi)$ — essentially linear in the number of trees times $\psi \log \psi$ of the single tree[17]. With $N=100$ and $\psi=256$, on datasets with millions of rows, training takes minutes rather than hours, and is easily parallelisable.
  • Inference per point: $O(N \log \psi)$. Negligible, can run in streaming.
  • Memory: linear in the number of trees and subsample points, independent of the original dataset. That's why it scales well to very large datasets.

4.4 Relevant variants

  • Extended Isolation Forest (Hariri, Kind, Brunner, 2019)[18]: corrects a branching bias of the original algorithm caused by axis-parallel splits, which create artificial "bands" of anomaly. Uses random non-axis-aligned hyperplanes. More robust, slightly slower.
  • SCiForest: variant with non-random split criteria. More precise but loses the computational simplicity advantage.
  • Conditional Isolation Forest: recent variant for data with temporal drift, useful when "normal behaviour" changes seasonally or over time (a typical case in banking transactions).

4.5 Implementation pitfalls

  • The contamination parameter in scikit-learn sets the alert threshold but not the score. Keep them conceptually separate: the score is an anomaly estimate, the threshold is a business decision to be tuned.
  • Feature engineering is critical. The algorithm is sensitive to feature choice. In AML, the most powerful features are aggregates over time windows (count/sum/std of transactions in the last 1/7/30/90 days), velocity ratios, distances from regulatory thresholds. Without these built upstream, the score loses meaning.
  • High-cardinality categorical features (IBANs, transaction reason codes) produce unstable results without proper encoding. Target encoding or embeddings are options; one-hot explodes the dimensionality.
  • The score is not a probability. Comparing scores across different datasets without retraining is methodologically incorrect.

Chapter FiveLayer 2 — Personalized PageRank

Once Layer 1 has selected suspicious entities, we need to know which other entities are connected to them. People who launder money rarely work alone: smurfers, money mules, transit accounts, nominees form graphs. An apparently clean entity strongly connected to risky ones deserves attention.

5.1 The algorithm

Personalized PageRank (PPR) is a variant of Google's PageRank[19]. The key difference is the personalisation vector $u$: instead of teleporting to a random node in the graph (standard PageRank), the random walker teleports to known high-risk entities — Layer 1 output, blacklists, prior STRs, sanctions. Risk propagates from seed nodes to their neighbours.

5.2 The iterative formula

$$v_{k+1} = \alpha \cdot M \cdot v_k + (1 - \alpha) \cdot u$$

Where:

  • $v$ is the vector of risk scores, one per node in the graph.
  • $M$ is the transition matrix built on the transaction graph. $M[i,j]$ is the probability that the random walker moves from node $j$ to node $i$, a normalised weight on the edge.
  • $u$ is the personalisation vector, high on seed nodes and zero elsewhere.
  • $\alpha$ is the damping factor, typically $0.85$. It's the probability that the walker follows a graph edge instead of teleporting to the vector $u$.
A note on the α convention Two equivalent but mirror-image conventions for $\alpha$ exist in the literature. The standard convention from NetworkX, scikit-network and GraphFrames uses $\alpha = 0.85$ as the damping factor. An alternative convention, common in parts of the academic literature, uses $\alpha \approx 0.15$ as the teleportation probability. The two are mathematically interchangeable (just swap $\alpha$ with $1-\alpha$), but if a development team mixes them up, the code can end up doing the opposite of what's intended. Worth fixing the convention once and documenting it in the repository.

5.3 Computational complexity

  • Power iteration: $O(|E| \cdot k)$ where $|E|$ is the number of edges and $k$ the number of iterations. Convergence is geometric with rate $\alpha^k$. Typically 50–100 iterations are enough for $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$. On a graph with $10^8$ edges, 100 iterations require $10^{10}$ operations: feasible in a nightly batch, not in real time.
  • Local approach (FwdPush, Andersen-Chung-Lang): complexity $O\left(\frac{1}{\alpha \epsilon}\right)$ independent of graph size[20]. Only explores the local neighbourhood of the seeds. This is the right approach when you have few seeds and want to query the graph on demand.
  • Memory: the vector $v$ has dimension $|V|$. For a graph with $5 \times 10^7$ nodes, a float64 vector takes 400 MB. Worth considering when computing many PPRs for many different seeds.

5.4 Why PPR works for AML

  • It captures relational contagion. An apparently clean entity surrounded by risky nodes ends up with an elevated score — exactly what we care about.
  • It naturally detects round-tripping. If flow cycles back to the origin node in a few hops, PPR amplifies the score across the entire cycle.
  • It works offline. Computed in batch on the aggregated graph, no real-time inference needed for v1.
  • It's explainable by construction. A node's score decomposes exactly into contributions from the seed nodes that reach it. It's an exact sum, not an approximation.

5.5 Honest limits

  • It depends on seed quality. If seeds are wrong, PPR amplifies the error rather than correcting it. Seed selection (Junction 1, see section 7) is a critical decision.
  • It doesn't handle temporality out of the box. Classic PPR sees the graph as static. To capture "these two nodes have been doing suspicious transfers over the last 30 days", you need temporal variants with exponential edge-weight decay.
  • It's sensitive to graph density. On dense graphs or graphs with super-hubs (payment gateways, for example), the score "spreads out" and loses discrimination. Hub filtering or non-uniform edge weighting are needed.

Chapter SixLayer 3 — A two-technique explainability stack

Layer 3 is the difference between a system that produces "risk score = 0.87" and one that produces: "customer X has a score of 0.87 because (a) they made 12 sub-threshold transfers in 5 days, contribution +0.31; (b) counterparty Y has been on a blacklist since February, contribution +0.28; (c) the flow returns to the origin account in 3 hops, contribution +0.18". Only the second form survives an audit.

Technically these are two distinct problems, handled by two different families of techniques. Keeping them conceptually — and implementationally — separate is one of the most important design choices.

6.1 Component A — SHAP for Layer 1's tabular features

SHAP (SHapley Additive Explanations) is the standard technique for explaining ML models that work on numerical features. It's based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory (Shapley, 1953[21]), applied to ML by Lundberg and Lee in 2017[22].

The idea: if a model's features are "players" cooperating to produce a score, what's the fair contribution of each? The Shapley value assigns to each feature its average contribution across all possible subsets of the other features.

$$\phi_i = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|! \cdot (n - |S| - 1)!}{n!} \cdot \left[ f(S \cup \{i\}) - f(S) \right]$$

Where $\phi_i$ is the contribution of feature $i$ to the score, $S$ is a subset of the other features, and $f(S)$ is the model's prediction when only features in $S$ are "active". The weighted sum over all subsets guarantees four axiomatic properties (efficiency, symmetry, additivity, dummy player) that make the Shapley value unique among possible attribution methods.

The three main implementations

  • KernelSHAP. Model-agnostic. Approximates the Shapley value by sampling subsets. Slow but universal: usable on any model. To explain a single alert accurately, expect seconds to minutes per instance.
  • TreeSHAP. Exact and polynomial-time for tree-based models (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, random forest, gradient boosting). Computes the true Shapley value in polynomial time by exploiting tree structure. When it can be used, it should be.
  • DeepSHAP. Variant for neural networks, based on DeepLIFT. Approximates the Shapley value on deep models. Worth considering if neural components show up in v2.

Documented implementation pitfalls

  • Correlated features. SHAP on models with strongly correlated features (typical in financial data: amount, frequency, standard deviation, concentration index are all related) produces ambiguous attributions. The distinction between TreeSHAP-interventional and TreeSHAP-observational becomes important. The latter is simpler but assumes feature independence, an assumption violated in AML.
  • Baseline choice. SHAP values are additive relative to a reference baseline. The choice (zero, mean, dataset median, conditional expectation) changes the interpretation. Document it.
  • Default visualisations ≠ compliance-grade. The waterfall plots from the shap library are useful for a data scientist's debugging, but they aren't the right format for a compliance report. What a compliance officer needs is sorted tables with numerical contribution, direction (positive/negative relative to the score), and percentage of total.

6.2 Component B — Explaining Layer 2: not with SHAP

SHAP is not the right technique for explaining a Personalized PageRank score. PPR is not a model that takes features as input: it's an iterative algorithm on a relational structure. Applying SHAP to a PPR is methodologically forced.

For Layer 2, the work is topological, across four complementary techniques:

  • Path decomposition. A node's PPR score decomposes exactly as the sum of contributions from walks reaching it from the seeds. It's an algebraic decomposition, not an approximation. Paths can be sorted by contribution and the top-K shown, each with its chain of nodes and edges.
  • Per-seed contribution analysis. For each seed in vector $u$, the PPR-from-seed can be computed separately, and you can show how much each seed contributes to the final score of a target node. It's a costly computation (as many PPRs as seeds), but parallelisable and cacheable.
  • Local subgraph extraction. For a target node, extract the 1-2-3 hop subgraph, with edge weights and node scores. This is the "visual report" attached to the STR. Useful tools: NetworkX + matplotlib for static exports, Cytoscape.js for interactive dashboard UIs.
  • Temporal decomposition. If the PPR variant used includes time-decay on edges, the score can be decomposed over time windows: "this node's score grew 40% over the last month due to new edges toward these seeds".

6.3 What comes out of Layer 3 — the alert dossier

Combining the contributions of Components A and B into a unified output is the real product of Layer 3. Every alert should produce a structured document with standardised sections. A possible layout:

  • Executive summary. Three lines automatically extracted from the top-3 SHAP contributions and top-2 PPR paths.
  • Layer 1 contributions. Sorted table: feature, observed value, contribution direction, percentage weight on the score.
  • Network context. Visualisation of the 3-hop subgraph. List of seeds that contributed to the PPR score, with weights.
  • Timeline. Recent relevant events — anomalous transactions, profile changes, prior alerts on the same subject.
  • Audit metadata. Input data hash, model version, timestamp, threshold parameters. So that the decision can be reproduced in audit.
A choice to make carefully The temptation to use an LLM to generate alert narratives in natural language is strong. In v1 of the system, it seems appropriate to avoid it: LLMs can hallucinate details not present in the data, they are non-deterministic (two executions of the same prompt produce different texts — problematic for audit), and they don't validate with objective metrics the way a classifier does. The conservative strategy is template-based generation: pre-written sentences with variable slots filled from the data. Traceable, deterministic, validatable. LLMs are worth considering for a v2, but with strict guardrails (constrained decoding, grammar-based output, retrieval-augmented generation with explicit citations).

Chapter SevenThe three junctions

The three layers work if they're connected well. The junctions are where the real design complexity hides, and where overall system quality most often gets compromised by inattention.

7.1 Junction 1 — from Layer 1 to Layer 2

Isolation Forest produces a continuous score $s \in [0, 1]$ for each entity. To initialise PPR, you need a personalisation vector $u$ that identifies the seeds. Three design choices interact:

  • Binary threshold vs soft seed. Binary threshold: $u_i = 1$ if $s_i \geq \tau$ for some threshold $\tau$, otherwise $u_i = 0$. Simple but loses information. Soft seed: $u_i = s_i / \sum_j s_j$ (normalised). Preserves signal gradation, makes the PPR "flatter". The soft approach tends to be preferable as a default.
  • Mixing with external seeds. A decision has to be made on whether $u$ contains only Layer 1 output or a mix with external lists: sanctions, PEPs, prior STRs, internal blacklists. An explicit mix with documented weights makes more sense than a single channel: for example $u = 0.5 \cdot u_{\text{IF}} + 0.5 \cdot u_{\text{blacklist}}$, with the weights tuned on a golden test set.
  • Refresh frequency. PPR is batch; running it daily or weekly is reasonable. This decision shapes the upstream data architecture: Kafka streaming for Layer 1, batch for Layer 2.

7.2 Junction 2 — combining the two scores

At the end of Layers 1 and 2, every entity has two scores: $s_{\text{iso}}$ and $s_{\text{ppr}}$. They have to be combined to produce an alert. Three options with explicit trade-offs:

  • Linear combination with tuned weights: $\text{score} = w_1 \cdot s_{\text{iso}} + w_2 \cdot s_{\text{ppr}}$, with $w_1 + w_2 = 1$ and weights calibrated on a golden set. Simple, explainable, recommended for the first version.
  • Logical-gate combination: alert only if both scores exceed a threshold. Reduces false positives more aggressively but risks missing cases where one of the two signals dominates.
  • Meta-classifier: a gradient boosting trained on $(s_{\text{iso}}, s_{\text{ppr}})$ plus other features. More powerful, but reintroduces an ML layer that itself needs an explainability pipeline. Consider for v2.

7.3 Junction 3 — from score to report

The system's final output isn't a numerical score. It's an alert dossier, with the structure described in section 6.3. It needs to be designed as a product, not as a technique. The three minimum outputs are:

  • Compliance-ready narrative, template-based.
  • Visualisation of the local subgraph, exportable to PDF.
  • Complete audit trail (input hash, model version, timestamp, parameters).

Chapter EightLimits and open questions

The architecture described above addresses part of the problem. Other parts remain open. It's honest to name them.

8.1 The data problem doesn't go away with an algorithm

All the technical analysis of the preceding sections assumes the existence of clean, deduplicated, normalised transaction data. In practice this isn't the starting point of any real project. Entity resolution — figuring out when two records refer to the same entity under different spellings, different systems, missing fields — is the most underrated and costly problem of an AML project. No sophisticated algorithmic architecture works around the fact that without clean data upstream, the sieve's layers turn into garbage-in/garbage-out.

Mature tools exist (Splink from the UK Ministry of Justice, Dedupe, Zingg for Spark, Senzing as a commercial option[23]), but they are projects in their own right. Underestimating them is the most common way to watch an AML pilot die before reaching production.

8.2 GNN explainability is still an open problem

The choice to stay away from GNNs in v1 of the system is pragmatic, not principled. GNNs are state of the art on many graph learning tasks, and AML-specific literature exists[13]. The constraint keeping them out is the maturity of explainability techniques (GNNExplainer, SubgraphX, PGExplainer) — they exist but are fragile, and translating their explanations into compliance language is an open research area. Version 2 of the system will likely integrate a GNN for complex patterns, with a dedicated explainability infrastructure. When this becomes feasible depends on the maturation of XAI techniques for GNNs, not on the maturation of GNNs themselves.

8.3 How far the architecture generalises

Everything described here is built on known laundering patterns (smurfing, layering, round-tripping, money muling). More sophisticated typologies — trade-based money laundering, cuckoo smurfing, schemes routed through opaque jurisdictions — need graph enrichment with data many obliged entities don't have (commercial data, invoice data, beneficial owner data from foreign registers). The proposed architecture is a foundation to build on, not a complete system that handles every AML typology.

8.4 Where I don't yet have a settled view

For the sake of intellectual honesty, here are the points where my study of the problem left me without a solid answer:

  • How well meta-classifiers (Junction 2) hold up in audit. Adding a gradient boosting on top of the two scores means having to explain that layer too. Techniques exist, but I haven't yet seen a public example of an AML system using a meta-classifier with SHAP on the meta-classifier itself that has survived inspection by a serious authority. It's territory to explore.
  • Optimal PPR damping values for AML. The standard $\alpha = 0.85$ comes from the web search context. Some works suggest different values for anomaly detection tasks on graphs, but there isn't consensus yet. Calibration needs to be empirical.
  • The real impact of AMLA's RTS. The technical standards AMLA produces will shape specific documentation, log, and validation requirements. Until they're published, any claim about "exactly what the regulator will want" is speculative.

8.5 The general point

What I've described here is an architecture consistent with the requirements one can read today in regulatory texts and technical papers. It isn't a product. It isn't a guarantee that its implementation, done in a specific context, will work. Building a real AML system requires many decisions that depend on jurisdiction, specific client, available data, budget and timing. What I'm trying to do with these notes is propose a defensible technical foundation, not a turnkey solution.
